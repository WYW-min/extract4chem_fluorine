48: polyimide aerogels are suitable as materials for preparing extravehicular activity (EVA) suits for planetary surface missions, flexible thermal protection systems (TPS) for entry-deceleration-landing (EDL) operations,[10] and a range of other space related applications. PI aerogel networks are comprised of poly-(amic acid) (PAA) polymers that are synthesized from reaction of dianhydrides with diamines, followed by a thermal imidization process.[11-13] The resultant aerogels are linear structures formed from physical interactions between polymer chains that can result in poor mechanical properties that are unsuitable for aerospace applications.[14-16]
49: 
50: Several pioneering studies have attempted to address the brittle nature of these aerogel materials, with multifunctional compounds and nanoparticles being incorporated as cross-linkers into the aerogels to afford 3D network structures that can better withstand applied stresses.[17-20] For example, Meador et al. have reported a series of PI aerogels that
51: 
52: were cross-linked with polyamines and polyisocyanates, including 1,3,5-triaminophenoxy benzene (TAB),[21] octa-(aminophenyl) silsesquioxane (OAPS),[22] and 1,3,5-benzenetricarbonyl trichloride (BTC).[23] Pei et al.[24] utilized trimethoxysilane grafted onto PI side chains to obtain cross-linked aerogels with small pore diameters, high surface areas, and excellent mechanical properties. Zuo et al.[25] have successfully prepared cross-linked PI aerogels containing chemically modified graphene oxide. These cross-linking agents can improve the mechanical properties of PI aerogels and limit shrinkage, resulting in aerogels with improved stability profiles that are well-suited to aerospace applications. However, many of the chemical cross-linkers used in these processes are economically unviable for the mass production of aerogels, while the complex synthetic protocols used for their preparation can severely limit their large-scale preparation. Therefore, the development of more efficient and commercially viable ways of improving the mechanical properties of aerogels is necessary to broaden the scope of their applications.

54: Several research teams have investigated the effect of modifying the composition of the aerogel by incorporating a fiber matrix to overcome mechanical fragility.[26-30] The fiber matrix plays a key role in generating voids within the aerogel matric, which serves to decrease the overall size of the aerogel
55: 
56: component of the composite. Glass fiber is a versatile ceramic material with a low thermal expansion coefficient that is widely used as a matrix for preparing aerogel composites, that exhibit excellent thermal insulation properties and high mechanical stability.[31-33] Importantly, fibers of these composites can be processed into porous structures with tailored densities and architectures that are particularly beneficial for impregnation of the precursor solution.
57: 
58: In this work, we have explored the potential of employing lightweight porous glass fibers as a reinforcement for ceramic fiber reinforced polyimide (PI) aerogel composites. The structures and properties of the polyimide aerogels were optimized by combining a flexible diamine,  $4,4^{\prime}$ -oxydianiline (ODA), in combination with a rigid diamine, p-phenylene diamine (PPDA). The backbone structures of the resultant aerogels displayed improved mechanical and thermal properties that are well-suited for the production of lightweight, heat resistant, high strength materials for aerospace applications.
