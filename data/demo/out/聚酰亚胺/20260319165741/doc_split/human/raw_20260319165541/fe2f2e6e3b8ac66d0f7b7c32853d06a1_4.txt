111: 
112: Figure 2 shows the complete characterization of ACAT. As presented in Figure 2a, the  ${}^{1}\mathrm{H}$  NMR spectrum of ACAT reveals the signal at  $5.50~\mathrm{ppm}$  indicating the  $\mathrm{NH_2}$  protons. Moreover, the signals around  $7.3 - 6.8~\mathrm{ppm}$  show the splitting of the aromatic protons. The FTIR spectrum of ACAT in Figure 2b shows the absorption band at 3300 and  $3200~\mathrm{cm}^{-1}$  corresponding to the  $\mathrm{NH_2}$  groups, while the vibrational bands for quinoid and benzenoid ring appears at 1600 and  $1500~\mathrm{cm}^{-1}$ , respectively. The peak at  $830~\mathrm{cm}^{-1}$  may be correlated to the substitution pattern of a 1,4-disubstituted benzene ring. Moreover, Figure 2c shows the molecular weight of ACAT with a molecular ion peak appeared at  $380(m / z)$  [63,64].

124: # 3.2. Characterization of EPAA and EPI
125: 
126: As shown in Figure 3, FTIR was used to characterize the structure of EPAA and EPI. The FTIR spectra of EPAA 3a showed a characteristic carboxylic and amide absorption band at 3250–3500 (N–H and O–H spectra), 1677 (acid C=O spectra), and 1641 (amide C=O spectra)  $\mathrm{cm}^{-1}$ . In addition, the peaks observed at 1596 and  $1503\mathrm{~cm}^{-1}$  indicated the presence of quinoid and benzenoid rings. On the other hand, EPI showed typical asymmetric and symmetric carbonyl group absorption bands at 1715 and  $1675\mathrm{~cm}^{-1}$ . Moreover, the absorption band of the carboxylic acid group completely disappeared, which indicated the complete conversion of EPAA to EPI.

135: # 3.3.1. X-ray Diffraction (XRD)
136: 
137: The XRD patterns clearly indicate that EPIS, EPIP, and EPIG had a crystalline nature as shown in Figure 4a. It can be seen that as compared to EPI and EPIS, EPIG exhibited four additional peaks at  $2\theta$  of  $38.01^{\circ}$ ,  $43.96^{\circ}$ ,  $64.50^{\circ}$ , and  $77.42^{\circ}$ . All these peaks were attributed to the standard Bragg reflection from (111), (200), (220), and (311) of the crystallographic planes of the face-centered cubic lattice. The intense peak at 38.1 indicated that the growth of gold was fixed in (111) direction. Likewise, the EPIP curve showed the crystal-plane peaks of (111), (200), and (220) of platinum metal at  $2\theta$  of  $39.5^{\circ}$ ,  $44.6^{\circ}$ , and  $64.9^{\circ}$ . Thus, we may infer that the composition of the respective material was as expected, and no impurities were found in the XRD pattern [57,65,66].

155: # 3.3.2. X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS)
156: 
157: The XPS analysis was employed to determine the composition of all the given samples as shown in Figure 4b. The XPS spectra of EPIS, EPIP, and EPIG clearly showed peaks for C, N, and O atoms, while the absence of any other peak indicated the purity of the given materials. The binding energy peak at  $287.82\mathrm{eV}$  arose from C 1 s, while the peak at  $399.8\mathrm{eV}$  corresponded to N 1 s. This peak was developed due to interactions with the N atom and respective nanoparticles. In addition, for EPIS the binding energy of  $\mathrm{Ag3d_{5 / 2}Ag3d_{3 / 2}}$  was found to be  $371.5\mathrm{eV}$  and  $365.6\mathrm{eV}$ , respectively, which were comparable to the values reported in the literature for bulk Ag crystals (368 eV, 374 eV) [67]. Similarly, in case of EPIP, peaks appeared at  $71.3\mathrm{eV}$  and  $74.6\mathrm{eV}$  for Pt  $4\mathrm{f}_{7 / 2}$  and  $4\mathrm{f}_{5 / 2}$ , respectively, corresponding to
158: 
159: the zero-oxidation state [68]. Moreover, the EPIG XPS signal for  $\mathrm{Au}4\mathrm{f}_{7 / 2}$  and  $\mathrm{Au}4\mathrm{f}_{5 / 2}$  was observed as doublets at 81.5 and  $85.3\mathrm{eV}$ , respectively, which clearly indicates the reduction of  $\mathrm{Au}^{3 + }$  into  $\mathrm{Au}^0$ , as the binding energy of  $\mathrm{Au}4\mathrm{f}_{7 / 2}$  for  $\mathrm{Au}^{+3}$  is  $86.5\mathrm{eV}$  [54].

165: # 3.4. Redox Capability of Materials Measured by Electrochemical CV Studies
166: 
167: The cyclic voltammograms (CV) of all EPIs are shown in Figure 5. As no redox peak was observed for bare ITO electrodes, it was used as a control for 5a. The CV curve for EPI 5b showed a relatively small peak of a redox current at  $100\mathrm{Acm}^{-2}$ . However, the doping of EPI with respective metal nanoparticles resulted in an increase in current density. For example, the current density for EPIS 5c was  $225\mathrm{Acm}^{-2}$ , which was 2.3 times higher than that for EPI, while the response for EPIP 5d increased by four times with a current density of  $400\mathrm{Acm}^{-2}$ . Subsequently, the highest response was observed for EPIG with a current density of about  $600\mathrm{Acm}^{-2}$ , as shown in Figure 5e. Thus, the redox capability of the as-prepared ITO electrode coated with electroactive materials was decreased in the following order:  $\mathrm{EPIG > EPIP > EPIS > EPI}$ .

172: # 3.5. Kinetic Parameters Study of CPE Modified with EPIG
173: 
174: To study the nature and kinetics of the electrochemical reaction, the effect of the scan rate on CV was determined on a CPE modified with EPIG was used in  $0.1\mathrm{M}$  PBS  $(\mathrm{pH} = 7)$  towards the oxidation of  $50~\mu \mathrm{M}$  UA as evident in Figure 6a. It can be seen that with an increasing scan rate, the current density increased from  $3\mu \mathrm{Acm}^{-2}$  to  $8.6\mu \mathrm{Acm}^{-2}$ . Furthermore, the peak potential shifted towards the positive side with the increasing scan rate. Figure 6b showed a linear relationship between the peak current  $\left(\mathrm{I_p}\right)$  and square root of the scan rates  $(\nu_{1 / 2})$ , and the regression equation was  $\mathrm{I_p}(\mu \mathrm{A}) = 11.147_{\nu 1 / 2}(\mathrm{V}_{S - 1})_{1 / 2} + 0.562$  with a correlation coefficient of  $\mathbb{R}^2 = 0.9958$ . Thus, it indicated that the UA oxidation process proceeds via a diffusion-controlled electron transfer reaction, which is generally observed in systems when the kinetics are fast. Additionally, to investigate the mechanism of UA
175: 
176: electrocatalysis on an EPIG, a graph was plotted between the peak potential  $(\mathrm{E_p})$  and logv as shown in Figure 6c. From the curve, it can be seen that  $\mathrm{E_p}$  was proportional to logv by the following linear equation  $\mathrm{E_p} = 0.1112\log (\nu) + 0.5346,\mathrm{R}^2 = 0.97548$  , which indicates that the reaction is irreversible. Hence, an anodic peak potential can be evidenced by [69]
177: 
178: $$
179: \mathrm {E p} = \frac {2 . 3 0 3 \mathrm {R T}}{2 (1 - \alpha) \mathrm {n a F}} \cdot \log \vartheta + k \tag {1}
180: $$
181: 
182: where  $R$  and  $F$  are the universal gas constant and Faraday constant, respectively;  $\alpha$  is the electron transfer coefficient;  $n_a$  is the number of electrons transferred; and  $T$  is the room temperature. The Tafel slope value calculated from Figure 6c was 0.222 V/decade, which shows that one electron transferred in a rate-determining step by assuming  $\alpha = 0.61$ .

193: # 3.6. Electrochemical Sensing of UA
194: 
195: CV and amperometric studies were used to determine the electrocatalytic activity of the respective material for the detection of UA. Figure 7a showed the CV response corresponding to a bare CPE and a CPE modified with EPI, EPIS, EPIP, and EPIP in  $0.1\mathrm{M}$  PBS  $(\mathrm{pH} = 7)$  at  $350\mathrm{M}$  UA concentration with scan rate of  $50\mathrm{mVs^{-1}}$ . As observed, the bare CPE 7a showed the smallest response with a current density of  $12.73\mathrm{uAcm^{-2}}$  at a more positive potential of  $459\mathrm{mV}$ , while, the current density for the CPE modified with EPI, EPIS, EPIP, and EPIG was found to be 28.10, 29.67, 32.68, and  $37.39\mathrm{uAcm^{-2}}$ , respectively. It may be conclude that UA sensitivity decreases in the following order  $\mathrm{EPIG > EPIP >}$ $\mathrm{EPIS > EPI}$ .

223: # 3.7. Differential Pulse Voltammetric Responses for Tertiary Mixtures of AA, UA, and DA
224: 
225: Differential pulse voltammograms (DPVs) were used [80] to determine the selectivity of designed electrochemical sensors. The experiment was carried out using a tertiary mixture of  $10\mathrm{M}$  of DA,  $10\mathrm{M}$  of AA, and  $10\mathrm{M}$  of UA for a bare CPE, a CPE modified with an EPI, EPIS, EPIP, and EPIG in  $0.1\mathrm{M}$  PBS  $(\mathrm{pH} = 7)$  in the range of  $-0.3$  to  $0.8\mathrm{V}$ , as shown in Figure 8. It can be seen that the bare CPE 8a showed poor selectivity for a tertiary mixture of AA/DA/UA. However, an improved and clearly distinguished signal can be seen for a CPE modified with EPI 8b, EPIS 8c, EPIP 8d, and EPIG 8e. The overall current value of a CPE modified with metal NPs increased as compared to a EPI due to electrocatalysis. The separation potential was also significantly improved. It showed good separation with three
226: 
227: oxidation peaks around  $10 - 50\mathrm{mV}$ ,  $160 - 180\mathrm{mV}$ , and  $300 - 340\mathrm{mV}$ , which corresponded to the anodic potentials of AA, DA, and UA respectively. Due to the excellent electrocatalysis of EPIG as caused by Au NPs, the overall sensing current value was increased, depicting excellent selectivity, good resolution and prominent voltammetric peaks.
